What is an IDE?

    IDE stands for Integrated Development Environment, it essentially packages all the basic tools a programmer needs to write, test, and debug code into one platform. There are DOZENS of IDE programs to choose from, but the one I will be reccomending to you is Visual Studio Code.

    Why?

    Visual Studio Code is OVERKILL for a new programmer to say the least. It contains thousands upon thousands of extensions that allow for extensive customization of an individual programmer's workspace.

    BUT, with the right tutorials and explanatnions, a newer programmer can set up Python easily and contain themselves to that workspace if they so chose. With introducing them to Visual Studio Code immediately, it will allow for the programmer to slowly branch out as they see fit.

    I personally have grown accustomed to many of it's useful extensions, such as Live Share that allows the programmer to work simultaneously with others to code remotely on a single file. These are things I have come to rely on as a beginner coder, and I hope that any who see this page can also get past the initial shock of so many options at once in order to harvest the long term benefits of those options.

What Are My Options?

There are 3 main paths to advance your programming knowledge:

Bootcamp

    The shortest but most difficult path, bootcamps are programs either completed remotely at home or in a physical location where beginner programmers will work dilligently to retain mass amounts of information in a short 6-8 month span.

    These camps often encourage team-based problem-solving, where people of different skillsets will be grouped and made to collaborate on a single high-difficulty project. However it is important to note that the strategy and intensity of these camps vary wildly between each instance.

    These programs are extreme, but have been proven to be an effective way to grow one's skills massively. If you feel as though you have the dedication required, I strongly encourage you at least attempt a shorter, 3-month camp.

College

    By far the most traditional and reliable path, a college degree is the most sure-fire way to guarantee high quality education and work after graduation. The most common issues with this path come with length and cost, even the cheapest New York SUNY degree costs over 27k per semester for an on-campus freshmen student. Obviously, these costs are cut by things such as Community College transfers, scholarships, and FAFSA student loans.

    There are many majors to choose from when taking this path:

  • Computer Science: The most common degree to attain for programmers, over 72% of Software Engineers hold this degree or one similar to it. Majoring in this will come with a main focus on software and theoretical discussion, with a side focus on mathematics.
  • Computer Engineering: If you are more interested in the hardware side of technology, this degree focuses on the technical side of CPUs, Memory Management, Graphics, and Internal Processing. This degree is generally considered more difficult, but can also be more awarding when it comes to finding internships or on-campus work.
  • Data Science: Out of all the majors listed, this one will be the most difficult. The courses for this degree focus on statistics, analytical data, database management, and how to conduct scientific studies. If you're interested in helping create advanced strategy models for businesses and solving scientific issues through analytics, this is the degree for you.

Self-Study

    This is the most relaxed path for any programmer to take, but also requires high levels of concentration and a desire to learn. Self-study will not guarantee you a job in a high-paying field, but can land you entry level positions in analytics and menial task automation.

    The most attractive benefit to this path is that it is free, as the internet is filled with resources such as Khan Academy, The Odin Project, and Automate The Boring Stuff.
